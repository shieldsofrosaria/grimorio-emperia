Chronicles of Lord Edmund Ashford

Being an Account of the Author & His Studies

~ Of the Author ~

I am Edmund Ashford, third son of House Ashford, whose ships have plied the trading routes between England and the Levant for three generations. My father expected me to follow my elder brothers into the family business‚Äîtallying manifests, negotiating contracts, and ensuring the profitable flow of spices, silks, and other luxuries to eager merchants. A respectable occupation, to be certain, and one that has kept our family in considerable comfort.

Yet I confess that whilst my brothers took naturally to the ledger and the counting house, I found myself far more captivated by the contents of our cargo than its value in coin. What manner of plant produced such fragrant cinnamon bark? By what process did Eastern artisans create their exquisite dyes? What wisdom lay behind the medicinal herbs our ships transported, known by names I could barely pronounce?

At the age of twenty, following a particularly heated disagreement with Father over a "perfectly good shipment of pepper" that I had inadvertently ruined through unsanctioned botanical experiments, we reached an understanding. He would grant me an allowance sufficient to pursue my peculiar interests, provided I ceased attempting to transform the family warehouse into a laboratory. I agreed with enthusiasm, packed my traveling trunk, and set forth to learn what I could from the wide world beyond our counting houses.

The years that followed took me from the universities of Bologna to the markets of Constantinople, from monastery libraries to the workshops of humble village healers. I learned that wisdom cares nothing for social standing‚Äîa Syrian grandmother taught me more about the properties of chamomile than any scholar I met at Oxford. An Egyptian scribe revealed to me the principles of Ma'at and the wisdom of Thoth. A Greek sailor, during a storm that tested our very souls, spoke of Caerus and the opportune moment with such clarity that I understood timing as I never had before.

Father once told me, "Edmund, you possess the resources to indulge your fancies, but that does not make them sensible." He was quite correct. I am acutely aware that my circumstances‚Äîwealth, education, freedom to travel‚Äîare extraordinary privileges. Not everyone can abandon commerce to chase after ancient gods and herbal remedies. Yet I hope that by documenting what I have learned, others might benefit regardless of their station. Knowledge, unlike spices, grows more valuable when freely shared.

These volumes represent years of study, experimentation, failure, and occasional success. I have tested every tea blend upon myself, sometimes with regrettable results (I do not recommend combining valerian with excessive amounts of ginger‚Äîthe evening was most unpleasant). I have burned numerous scrolls in the name of banishment, including one memorable incident where I nearly set fire to an inn in Bruges (the proprietor was remarkably understanding once I explained it was for empirical research).

What you hold, dear reader, is not the work of a singular genius but rather a compilation of wisdom gathered from countless teachers, translated from numerous languages, tested with the rigor of one trained in merchant precision, and organized according to principles I have found both practical and meaningful. The Three Virtues‚ÄîCaerus, Thoth, and Ma'at‚Äîprovide the philosophical foundation. The Rede serves as our ethical compass. The practices detailed herein have been refined through years of dedicated application.

I write not as a mystic or a priest, but as an empirical philosopher. Each working described has been tested. Each correspondence noted has been observed. Where uncertainty exists, I have noted it. Where practices failed, I have documented why. This is scholarship in the classical sense‚Äîthe careful observation and recording of phenomena, undertaken with both rigor and wonder.

My hope is that these studies prove useful to you, whether you approach them as a fellow scholar, a curious experimenter, or simply someone seeking practical wisdom for daily life. Use what resonates, adapt what does not, and above all, maintain your own documentation. The empirical method demands nothing less than honest observation and careful record-keeping.

A final note on the structure of these volumes: I have organized the material to progress from foundational philosophy through daily practices to more complex workings. Each volume builds upon the last, though one need not master Volume I before finding value in Volume II. I myself often reference Volume V whilst preparing my morning tea from Volume II, and I suspect you shall develop your own patterns of use.

The forewords to each volume contain my personal reflections on their development and application. I have tried to be forthright about my own struggles and breakthroughs, as I believe such honesty serves the reader better than false authority. I am, after all, still learning‚Äîa student of these studies as much as their chronicler.

With sincere wishes for your own enlightenment,

Edmund Ashford
Lord of the House Ashford
Empirical Philosopher & Perpetual Student

Written in the Year of Grace 1347
At my studium in the city of Avignon
